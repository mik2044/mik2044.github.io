Through the analysis of 14,106 whole tumor genomes from the Genomics England project, we discovered that the mitochondrial ribosomal RNA genes MT-RNR1 and MT-RNR2 are recurrent targets of somatic hotspot mutations in cancer, with ~4% of tumors harboring at least one rRNA hotspot. These hotspots preferentially affected germline-constrained, evolutionarily conserved positions and clustered structurally at mRNA channel and tRNA-contacting sites within the mitoribosome. Using precision mtDNA base editing, we engineered the exemplar hotspot m.1227G>A and performed proteomics, isotope-tracing metabolomics, and single-cell multiome sequencing to find heteroplasmy-dependent loss of respiratory chain subunits, diminished respiration, and distinctive metabolic rewiring characterized by increased glycolytic activity, all manifesting at heteroplasmies as low as 10%. Single-cell analysis confirmed progressive downregulation of mtDNA-encoded and OXPHOS genes with threshold effects at 20-30% heteroplasmy, well below the ~60% threshold typical of functionally recessive mtDNA disease variants. These findings establish somatic mutations of mitochondrial ribosomal RNA as the first class of functionally dominant mtDNA mutations in cancer, challenging the prevailing model that pathogenic mtDNA mutations require high heteroplasmic burden to exert phenotypic effects.
