Using amplification-free single-cell whole-genome sequencing (DLP+, Direct Library Prep+), we profiled mtDNA copy number, mtDNA genotype, and nuclear DNA copy number across 72,275 single cells spanning cell lines, patient-derived xenografts, and primary human tumors. We found that per-cell mtDNA copy number varies extensively and scales with cell size, consistent with anabolic demand-driven mitochondrial biogenesis. Whole-genome doubling events in the nuclear genome triggered stoichiometrically proportional increases in mtDNA copy number, preserving the mtDNA-to-nuDNA ratio (MNR). This suggests that cells maintain dosage homeostasis between the two genomes through both passive and active mechanisms. Notably, clonal diversification could perturb MNR levels independently of ploidy, and multimodal integration of DLP+ with scRNA-seq revealed that high-MNR clones upregulated interferon response and mtDNA-encoded OXPHOS genes while depleting hypoxic gene expression. Finally, we demonstrated that both germline non-coding polymorphisms and somatic truncating mutations in mtDNA elicit heteroplasmy-dependent changes in mtDNA copy number at single-cell resolution. Together, these findings implicate the coregulation of mitochondrial and nuclear genomes as a previously underappreciated axis of phenotypic variation and cellular fitness in cancer.
